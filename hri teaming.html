Research Overview

This project explored the dynamics of human-robot collaboration through a game-based research environment combining Coactive Design theory, controlled experimentation, and user-centred interface redesign to answer three core research questions about human-machine teaming.

❓

RQ1 - Teaming Dynamics

To what extent do human-machine teaming dynamics in the game influence teamwork, user experience, and usability?

⚙️

RQ2 - CoAD Methods

How can different Coactive Design methods impact human-machine teaming dynamics, and what happens when Standard vs. Modified CoAD are compared?

🖥️

RQ3 - UI Redesign

How can the game's user interface be redesigned using UCD principles to optimise usability and enhance overall player experience?

UI Redesign - UCD Process

A user-centred design process was applied to the existing game interface, moving from pain-point identification through Figma wireframing and prototyping to a redesigned interface validated against Nielsen's heuristics.

✗ Existing UI - Pain Points Identified
1 ~⅓ of screen occupied by unused black area - major viewport waste
2 Incorrect robot names displayed in UI - Blue Robot labelled as Red
3 Suboptimal minimap and button placement - hindered quick scanning
4 Large text-label buttons consumed critical game viewport space
5 No semi-transparency - buttons fully obscured game content beneath
✓ Redesigned UI - Improvements Delivered
1 Full-screen game viewport - black area eliminated entirely
2 Icon-based semi-transparent buttons - minimal footprint, max visibility
3 Consistent colour-coding per robot - blue/red/pink always distinct
4 Minimap relocated to top-right - glanceable situational awareness
5 Scores prominently displayed at top - team performance always visible

07

Novel Contributions

1 - Empirical Evidence for Visibility-Driven HMT Dynamics

Systematic comparison of light and dark game versions produced empirical evidence for how environmental uncertainty modulates communication patterns, performance, and perceived interdependence in human-AI teams, extending prior CoAD literature into interactive environments.

2 - Custom Human-Machine Teaming (HMT) Questionnaire

Developed and validated a 10-item, 7-point Likert instrument measuring perceived collaboration, trust, and interdependence in human-AI teams. Adaptable for future HRI studies across domains beyond gaming.

3 - Modified CoAD Method with Quantitative Scoring

Extended the Standard Coactive Design method with a numerical scoring system, communication flow analysis, information requirements mapping, and explicit automation-level comparison, providing concrete tools that the standard method lacked.

4 - UCD-Grounded Interface Redesign Model

Demonstrated how established HCI principles (consistency, visibility, feedback, minimalism) can be systematically applied to collaborative AI game interfaces, producing a replicable redesign process for human-robot interaction systems.

08

Reflections & Future Directions

📡

Communication ≠ Collaboration

The dark version generated 69% more messages but worse team outcomes. The lesson: designing for communication quantity is a proxy metric, shared situational awareness is the actual target variable.

⚖️

The Autonomy Trade-off

Version 3 (semi-autonomous) consistently outperformed both fully manual and highly autonomous configurations, supporting the meta-analytic finding that mid-level automation preserves human engagement while leveraging robot capability.

🎮

Challenge Can Enhance Experience

Despite lower SUS scores, the dark version produced higher Overall UEQ ratings. Difficulty and friction are not inherently negative; they become negative only when they undermine agency rather than augmenting it.

Future Research Directions Identified
Adaptive AI systems that personalise autonomy levels to individual cognitive profiles and skill levels in real-time
Explainable AI interfaces that surface robot decision-making reasoning to build calibrated trust in teammates
Larger sample studies (n>50) to achieve statistical power sufficient for ANOVA-based hypothesis testing across all HMT dimensions
VR/AR-based HMT environments that enable physical-digital robot interaction extending Gerling et al.'s physical game UI findings
